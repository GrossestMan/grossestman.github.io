DASH is a fast-paced action-platformer where your sole movement mechanic is an aerial dash attack. Rated “Best in Show” by IndieHangover.com at the 2014 Boston Festival of Indie Games, DASH won the 'Most Polished Game' and 'Audience Favorite' awards at Cornell's annual Game Design Initiative Showcase and is the highest-rated game ever developed through the game design program. Built with LibGDX for Desktop, Android, and iOS, DASH is set for release on iOS and Android this summer (2015).

As project lead, I am involved in all aspects of the game; from code to level design and polish to marketing, I make sure everything runs smoothly.

To design levels, we built a custom built level editor with d3 to make any level imaginable. Each level designer has their own technique, but I usually follow the same creative process:

  1. Pray for creative juices to start flowing
  1. Jot down ideas on types of challenges to be included, which emotions I'm trying to play off the player (eg. fast-paced = highly focused, tense, alert), and which secondary objectives may fit well with the level
  2. Sketch out core challenge on pen and paper
  3. Translate core challene into editor
  4. Playtest level
  5. Make necessary adjustments, or possibly scrap level entirely and return to step 2
  6. Add rest of level
  7. Send level through peer review process
  8. Make final adjustments before testing and polish obstacles, enemy placement, and other level elements
  9. Playtest on friends and Beta testers
  10. Finalize level

Each level has an artifact (the token with blue flames) strategically placed in a hard to reach area that can help you unlock new worlds quicker. While not required to complete a level, they add an extra layer of difficulty to each level for the more skilled players, who get rewarded with an extra game mode once they 'catch em all'. I made the following level look deceivingly easy, but it takes some strategy to figure out. First, some background: your sole movement mechanic is dashing at targetables (enemies, lanterns, artifacts) by tapping/clicking on them. You collect artifacts by simply passing through them, but you drop them in place if you are hit by an enemy. The shielded enemies block the entrance from the right side and will 'punch' you with their shields if you get too close. Also, the blue birds on the top left charge up a projectile fireball that they shoot at you. Now, the player isn't given the inside scoop like I just gave you, but see if you can figure out what to do.

DASH has 4 gorgeous landscapes with 5 unique enemy types and lots of twists and turns along the way. We're getting pumped for a big release this summer as it is the result of over a year's hard work by the entire Speedy Chalupa crew: Sofonias Assefa, Karen Zhou, Robin Martin, Roger Chen, Steven Mah, and myself.

FreshAir is an allergy detection and alert system that maps common airborne allergens via a crowdsourced network of sensors. I designed the app interface along with Emily Lutz and Maddy Butzbach as part of INFO 3450 - Human Computer Interaction.

Our task was to solve a problem facing the world today by designing a novel system utilizing any technology that would be feasible within the next 10 years. After an interesting brainstorming session where we tossed around over 100 ideas (two words: bubble boy), we chose to tackle seasonal allergies through a detection and alert system composed of a wearable sensor and a mobile app. The sensors would be carried at all times to gather continuous air quality data throughout the day, which would be aggregated and transformed into an interactive map with overlays depicting the locations of relevant allergens.

We broke up potential users into four personas:

  1. Mild allergy sufferer
  2. Severe allergy sufferer
  3. Asthma-sufferer from polluted area
  4. Non-allergy sufferer

We asked them all sorts of questions such as What is your worst experience/memory related to allergies? and What measures do you take to prevent and/or stop allergy attacks from happening? to find out when, where, and how often they are affected by allergies/pollution as well as their degree of adversion to discover current motivations and techniques for handling them. We thought it would be interesting to interview a non-allergy sufferer as well to see if they had any additional insight or could think of other potential uses for this kind of system. After interviewing people fitting these personas, we constructed a Work Affinity Activity Diagram to group common themes from different responders. Through persona interviews, we found that people would not be willing to carry around a clunky sensor, especially since it had to be exposed at all times. We interviewed a wearable technology expert at Cornell University who said that although these types of sensors are not quite small enough today, the technology should evolve to the point where they can be accessorized into earrings, necklaces, and bracelets. This way, people could make a fashion statement by equipping everyday accessories with stylish, high-tech sensors.

From here, I storyboarded 'A day in the life of John' (our allergy sufferer), and we each created low-fidelity mockups of proposed concept designs.

×

We thought it would be cool to add in a glasses component that would highlight affected areas through a colored overlay projected directly through the lens. It would take in the same data used for the map to cover your entire field of vision so that as you look around, you can see exactly where the affected areas are. This technology would ideally integrate directly with any pair of glasses you owned, and the overlay feature could be turned on/off to the user's liking. After performing concept validation on our test personas, the glasses and the map proved popular. They liked how the glasses provided immediate information about their surroundings, which complemented the ability to plan routes around affected areas in advance by using the map.

After finalizing our concept, we drew sketches and wireframes of the app and produced high fidelity mockups in Photoshop and Illustrator to put together an interactive prototype using InVision. You can view our poster for presentations here.

As part of INFO 3300 - Data Driven Web Applications, I created a visualization of passenger survival rate on the Titanic based on demographic data incuding age, social class, price of ticket, and gender. Passengers are broken up into 3 tiers based on their social class, with upper class on the top, descending to lower class on the bottom.
