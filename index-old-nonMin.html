Epic: Product

Epic makes medical record software that holds patient records for over 58% of Americans. I worked closely with surgery product (OpTime) leadership to lead product initiatives and design new features.

Launched Upgrade Tool Saving 10,000+ Client Hours

There was a critical visual issue in the 2017 release discovered a few weeks before the first client upgrade. Each client was uniquely affected by a change to the system's icons and we needed a quick solution. I was brought on to interface with the design teams and clients closest to the issue.

After analyzing client usage data, I found that the average client only used 5% of available icons in the system, so I proposed building an identification and prioritization tool to narrow issue scope. I quickly designed and launched the tool with support engineers from our first client upgrades before rolling it out across all upgrading customers. The tool worked very well for OpTime clients, so I expanded the scope across all clinical applications and created a process for all support engineers to follow.

In the end, we collectively saved over 10,000 client hours during their busy upgrade season.

Led Visual Redesign of Epic OpTime

There was a company-wide initiative to modernize the look and feel of Epic's software in the 2016 release. I led the project to redesign the OpTime product.

I recruited a QA team to discover product layout, formatting, and style enhancements based on the new design guidelines. As the enhancements came in, I actively triaged and delegated them based on impact and development effort. Since OpTime is a very integrated product, I had to manage development effort across many product teams.

In the end, we made hundreds of visual enhacements to produce a completely redesigned OpTime product in the 2016 release.

FreshAir

Seasonal allergies affect over 40 million Americans every year. My project team for INFO 3450 - Human Computer Interaction wanted to create something that helped people cope with indoor and outdoor allergies, so we designed an allergy identification and alert system called FreshAir. I designed the app interface along with Emily Lutz and Maddy Butzbach.

How It Works

The system uses a crowdsourced network of wearable and deployed sensors to continuously monitor air quality. Through persona interviews, we found that many people do not know what they are allergic to, and therefore what to avoid. By documenting allergic reactions in the FreshAir app, the system can identify what you are allergic to so you are better prepared both in the household and outdoors.
